What is root canal therapy? Read more

The need for root canal treatment arises for two main reasons:

Infection or permanent damage to the tooth’s pulp: Commonly this is the result of an untreated cavity, which allows the bacteria to enter and infect the pulp. Inflammation resulting from such infection restricts the tooth's blood supply. Hence antibiotic treatment is not effective in treating it.

Trauma or dental restorative work: Trauma and extensive dental restorative work can also result in the need for root canal treatment.

Signs and symptoms of pulp infection
In the case of pulp infection, no pain is felt at first. Pain and swelling occur if the infection is left untreated. An absess may develop. The following are common signs and symptoms of pulp infection:

  • Touching, pushing, or biting down on a tooth makes results in pain
  • Tooth is sensitive to heat and cold for more than a few seconds
  • Swelling near the tooth
  • Discoloration of tooth (with or without pain)
  • Broken tooth

The pulp consists of blood vessels, nerves and various other tissues. While the pulp is vital in a tooth’s development during childhood, by the time we reach adulthood, the tooth has finished growing and the pulp starts to decrease in size. If the pulp becomes infected or contaminated by decay, the tooth becomes painful and root canal treatment may be necessary.

Associated risks

Root Canal Therapy or Other Necessary Treatment

Once any prior fillings and decay are removed, it may be revealed that there is a more severe condition present that will need to be treated. A root canal procedure may be necessary before placing the crown to ensure the tooth continues to function without issue. In some cases, the tooth may be deemd unrestorable making a crown no longer an option.

Pulp Death Requiring Root Canal Therapy

Ocassionaly the nerve of a tooth dies following the placement of a crown. Should this occur, root canal therapy or extraction are the only options.

Gum Recession

Gums can recede with age, therefore exposing the margins of the crown. This can be unsightly and may require the replacement of the crown in the future. Such a replacement will be at my expense.

Patients That Have Existing Root Canal Teeth

At the time of crowning the tooth, the root canal appeared infection free. However, with all root canal treated teeth there is a risk of possible reinfection of the tooth. If this was to occur the options would be to either have the tooth re-treated by a specialist (Endodontist) or to have the tooth removed.

Patients that have an existing denture around the fitting surface of the crown

It can be very difficult to design a new crown that will fit perfectly around an existing denture. We take extra steps to ensure that your denture will fit as closely as possible to the new crown. Unfortunately, in some cases this is not possible. We have the ability to make minor adjustments to both the crown and the denture. However, occasionally this is not sufficient and a new denture will be necessary at your own cost.

Caring for the Restoration

As part of the treatment, I may be required to wear temporary crowns for several weeks while waiting for my final restoration to be fabricated. During this time, I must be careful to ensure the temporary restoration remains in place and does not fall off. I also understand that once treatment is started, I must return to have the treatment finished at the recommended time. Failure to do so may result in decay, the need for root canal treatment, tooth fracture or loss of the tooth. Further necessary treatment due to failure to keep appointments will be at an additional cost. Like natural teeth, crowns and bridges need to be kept clean with a proper oral hygiene routine, otherwise decay may develop underneath or around the restoration, leading to further treatment and possibly a replacement of the crown(s) or bridge.

What is a crown? Read more

A crown is basically a manufactured tooth. It’s generally made of porcelain or ceramic, which can be matched to the colour and texture of your existing teeth.

A crown can also be made of gold alloys for improved strength when replacing a back tooth. Often a metal crown will have porcelain bonded to the exterior to give it strength and a natural look.

What are direct and indirect fillings? Read more

Depending on your unique case, you may require an indirect or a direct dental restoration. What is the difference? An indirect restoration will require more than a single visit. At the first visit, you’ll have the tooth prepared and an impression will be taken of the restoration area. When you visit the dentist again, the filing will then be placed and any needed adjustments can be made. Fillings used for indirect restorations include indirect composites, gold alloys, ceramics and base metal alloys.

When it comes to a direct dental restoration, you’ll only need to make one visit to the dentist. The filling can be placed into the prepared cavity right away. The tooth is prepared and the filling placed and then any adjusting is done.

What is oral sedation? Read more

Oral sedation is the most common form of antianxiety medication used for majority of patients. The choice of drug, dosage and timing is decided by one of our trained sedation dentists who can also prescribe you the medication at the time of your consult appointment.

If taken 1 hour prior to appointment the oral sedation pill will take effect almost immediately, helping to calm and relax you and keep you unaware as your Healthy Smiles dentist works on your mouth.

What is a tooth extraction? Read more

Tooth extraction is the removal of a tooth. A tooth may need to be extracted for a number of reasons including extensive damage, disease to the surrounding gums (periodontitis), problems with eruption such as Wisdom Teeth and also to prevent further problems from arising if a tooth is diseased or infected. Teeth may also sometimes be extracted as part of orthodontic treatment to improve the effectiveness of treatment.

Associated risks

Pain

Pain can occur in the Jaw due to irritation of the nerves and the movement of the joint (TMJ) during the extraction. This pain is usually temporary and can often be treated with most available pain relief medicines.

Dry Socket

A dry socket can occur when a blood clot does not correctly form in the remaining socket after extraction. The bone underneath the gum can be exposed to air or food and can be very painful. Dry sockets will need to be treated separately.

Infection

The remaining socket can become infected and must be kept clean to ensure a low risk of infection. This is likely to resolve completely once treatment is undertaken but if infection continues to occur it can usually be treated with antibiotics.

Tooth Fracture

It is possible that during extraction, the tooth may fracture, particularly at the root. The remaining fragment of root may be left in place if the dentist deems it too risky to remove such as in the case where it may lie close to a nerve.

Nerve Damage

During extraction, underlying nerves may lie close to the tooth that needs to be extracted. Despite all precautions taken, nerves can be damaged and numbness or loss of feeling and use of the affected area can occur. The effects of this can be temporary if the nerve is irritated during surgery, or permanent if significant damage occurs to the nerve. Areas that can be affected by this include the lips, chin, cheeks, tongue and surrounding teeth.

Option 3: Do nothing

Total $0.00

Some people choose to do nothing especially if they are not in pain. But it is important to know the risks of leaving the infection in your mouth.

There is a risk that an infection can develop into an abscess, which can in extremely rare cases be fatal. Secondly if it doesn’t become an abscess and it periodically drains by itself your body will still be compromised in its immune system through constantly fighting infection. Thirdly you could be limiting your option of replacing that tooth over time. The infection can eat away at the surrounding bone and leave you with less area to place an implant into making it a much longer and costly process.
